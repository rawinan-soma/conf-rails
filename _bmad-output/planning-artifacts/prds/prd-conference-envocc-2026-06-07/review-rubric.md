# PRD Quality Review — ENVOCC Conference Room Booking System

## Overall verdict

This is a tight, internally consistent PRD that knows exactly what it is: a security-driven full replacement of a fragmented two-app booking + registration system for a single organization. Its strongest assets are done-ness clarity and downstream usability — FRs are granular, ID-stable, and cross-referenced through a coherent role model that resolves the hard edge cases (FR-094 RBAC, FR-063 room-deactivation cascade, F11 internal vs. external registration). The main risk is not in what's written but in what's left implicit: the PRD reads as a settled spec with no Non-Goals callouts, no `[ASSUMPTION]` index, and no `[NOTE FOR PM]` markers, yet it carries inherited assumptions (e.g., "resolves OQ-3" referencing open questions that never appear in this document) and a few NFRs still phrased as adjectives rather than bounds. For a green-light-to-build artifact (status: final) this is mostly fine, but the absent open-items scaffolding means a reader cannot see where judgment was exercised versus where the user actually confirmed.

## Decision-readiness — adequate

The PRD states decisions as decisions, not considerations, and that is its decision-readiness strength. The most contentious design choices are made explicitly and defended: meal-type ownership is pushed to the registrant ("Meal-type selection is owned by the registrant, not the organizer," FR-021); admins are stripped of approval authority in three separate places (§3, FR-094, Constraints) precisely because that is the kind of thing a stakeholder would expect and the PRD wants no ambiguity; the resent-confirmation flow names the security trade-off it accepts ("To avoid disclosing whether an email is registered, the page shows the same neutral acknowledgement," FR-047). These are real decisions with the road-not-taken visible.

Where it is merely adequate rather than strong: there is no Open Questions section, no `[NOTE FOR PM]` callout anywhere, and the one place the PRD gestures at a resolved tension — "**Profile population (resolves OQ-3)**" (FR-095) — references an open-question artifact (OQ-3) that does not exist in this document. A reader cannot find OQ-1, OQ-2, or OQ-3, so the resolution is unverifiable in context. The PRD also smooths over at least one genuine tension without flagging it: FR-032 imposes no registrant cap while FR-061 keeps a room `capacity` field that the Constraints section demotes to "informational only" — a defensible call, but one a facilities stakeholder would push back on, and the PRD does not acknowledge the objection.

### Findings
- **medium** Dangling reference to OQ-3 with no Open Questions section (FR-095) — The phrase "resolves OQ-3" implies a tracked open question, but no OQ list exists in the PRD, so the resolution cannot be audited and the prior tension is invisible. *Fix:* Either add a short "Resolved Questions" subsection restating OQ-1..OQ-3, or drop the OQ-3 reference and state the decision plainly.
- **low** Uncapped-registration vs. room-capacity tension not surfaced as a decision (FR-032, FR-061, Constraints "Rooms are uncapped") — The choice to keep an informational `capacity` field while never enforcing it is reasonable but is presented as settled fact, not as a trade-off a stakeholder might contest. *Fix:* Add a one-line `[NOTE FOR PM]` acknowledging that capacity is intentionally non-binding and why (over-subscription accepted for these event types).

## Substance over theater — strong

Almost nothing here reads as furniture. There is no persona gallery — §3 defines exactly three roles (internal user with dual capacities, admin, external registrant) and every one drives concrete FRs, so there is no persona theater. There is no Differentiation/Innovation section at all, which is the correct restraint for an internal replacement tool; the PRD did not manufacture novelty it doesn't have. The Problem Statement is specific to ENVOCC ("two separate applications," "unsupported frameworks with active security vulnerabilities") and could not be swapped into another PRD unchanged, so it is not vision theater.

The one soft spot is NFR phrasing (handled in detail under Done-ness): NFR-001 and NFR-002 are genuine product-specific thresholds, but they sit close to boilerplate territory in wording. Overall the content is earned, not decorative.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The PRD has a clear thesis and the features follow from it: *unify two fragmented apps into one secure organizer workflow, give external attendees a real self-service channel, and give admins visibility without gatekeeping power.* That thesis is visible in the §1 bullets and is then enforced structurally — the dual-capacity account model (§3), the single unified booking form (FR-010), and the explicit "admins have no approval authority" stance all serve the "unify + de-gatekeep" arc rather than reading as an arbitrary feature list.

Success Metrics validate the thesis rather than measuring vanity activity: "Organizer task completion (full booking flow) ≥ 80% without assistance" and "External registration completion rate ≥ 90%" and "time-to-complete ≤ 2 minutes" all test the unification/self-service bet directly; "Double-booking incidents 0" tests the reliability promise. There is no DAU/MAU filler. The MVP scope kind is coherently a problem-solving/replacement scope, and the Out-of-Scope list (waitlist, templates, filtering, feedback forms, invite-only) is exactly the set of "nice but not the thesis" items, which reinforces the arc. No counter-metrics are stated — the only gap worth noting in an otherwise coherent metrics set.

### Findings
- **low** No counter-metrics named alongside Success Metrics (§2) — e.g., a guardrail that the ≥90% external completion target isn't bought by collecting too little data or by an over-permissive flow. *Fix:* Add one counter-metric (e.g., registration error/abandonment rate, or support requests for lost confirmation emails) so the targets aren't gamed.

## Done-ness clarity — adequate

The functional side is strong. Most FRs carry a testable consequence: FR-004/NFR-002 give a binary, verifiable condition (no overlapping double-booking, 100%); FR-037 fixes the reminder to exactly "1 day before the event ... a single fixed reminder — there are no multiple intervals"; FR-041/FR-041a enumerate exact fields and the conditional "Other" reveal; FR-082 tabulates every transactional email by trigger and recipient. The role and registration edge cases (FR-063 cascade with a confirmation warning, FR-094's three RBAC clauses, F11's in-app-vs-email confirmation distinction) are specified to a level a story author can act on with little back-and-forth. The §2 metrics table even supplies acceptance-grade thresholds for the headline flows.

The weakness is concentrated in the NFRs, where the rubric says to be unforgiving. NFR-003 says load "within 3 seconds under normal organizational load" — the 3s is a bound but "normal organizational load" is undefined (how many rooms, concurrent organizers, registrants?), so the test is not reproducible. NFR-001 "no known critical or high-severity vulnerabilities at launch" is verifiable against a scanner, but "stored securely" is an adjective with no bound. NFR-005 ("retained indefinitely until explicitly deleted by an admin") is clear. NFR-004 is actually good — it names equal mobile/desktop priority and "no horizontal scrolling or zoom," which is testable. There is no dedicated Acceptance Criteria section, which is acceptable here because the FR consequences and the metrics table largely carry it — but the NFR softness should be tightened before architecture leans on it.

### Findings
- **high** "Normal organizational load" is undefined (NFR-003) — A 3-second bound is meaningless without the concurrency/volume it holds at; downstream architecture and performance testing cannot size against it. *Fix:* State the load envelope (e.g., N active rooms, P concurrent organizers, Q registrants/min on a registration page) the 3s target must hold under.
- **medium** "Stored securely" is an adjective, not a bound (NFR-001) — Leaves encryption-at-rest, hashing, and token storage to interpretation on a PRD whose entire premise is security. *Fix:* Specify the mechanism (e.g., credentials hashed with a named algorithm, tokens stored hashed/short-lived, transport over TLS) or explicitly defer the detail to the architecture spec by reference.
- **low** No explicit acceptance section, relying on FR consequences (§4–§5) — Works for the well-specified FRs but means the NFRs have no pass/fail line of their own. *Fix:* Either accept (FR consequences suffice) or add a one-line acceptance note per NFR.

## Scope honesty — thin

This is the dimension where the PRD's "final/settled" posture works against it. The Out-of-Scope (Post-MVP) section in §7 is genuinely useful and does real work — it lists seven concrete deferrals (waitlist, templates, calendar-filter, heatmap filtering, heatmap tooltips, feedback form, invite-only) rather than hand-waving. That part is good.

But the rest of the scope-honesty scaffolding the rubric looks for is absent: there are zero `[ASSUMPTION: …]` tags and no Assumptions Index, zero `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` inline callouts, and zero `[NOTE FOR PM]` markers — yet the PRD clearly rests on inferences (e.g., identity-provider is OIDC per FR-095, 30-minute session default per FR-093, "normal organizational load," indefinite retention). On a settled internal-tool PRD a low open-items density is appropriate, but *zero* assumption tags combined with a dangling "resolves OQ-3" reference suggests the open items were resolved and then erased rather than recorded. A reader cannot distinguish "the user confirmed this" from "the PM inferred this and it seemed safe." The defaults are reasonable, so this is thin rather than broken — the risk is auditability, not correctness.

### Findings
- **medium** No Assumptions Index and no inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags despite clear inferred defaults (FR-093 30-min timeout, FR-095 OIDC sourcing, NFR-003 load, NFR-005 retention) — A downstream reader can't tell confirmed requirements from PM inferences. *Fix:* Tag the inferred defaults inline and collect them in an Assumptions Index; mark any the user explicitly confirmed as confirmed.
- **low** No `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` inline callouts where silent omission is possible (e.g., no waitlist behavior when an event is "full," no recurring bookings, no calendar sync stated only in Constraints) — §7 covers most, but Constraints and FRs could each carry a pointer. *Fix:* Add brief inline non-goal pointers at the FRs most likely to invite "but what about…" (FR-032 capacity, FR-014 duplication vs. templates).

## Downstream usability — strong

This PRD is built to be source-extracted. FR IDs are unique and the cross-references resolve cleanly and pervasively — FR-040↔FR-095 (contact auto-population), FR-021↔FR-041a (meal type), FR-063↔FR-082 (cascade emails), F11 throughout (FR-100..FR-105) pointing back to FR-022/035/036/082. Cross-references use specific FR IDs rather than "see above," so each section stands largely on its own. Domain nouns are used consistently — "registered attendees," "owning organizer," "external registrant," "internal user," "capacity" (in both its role and seating senses, disambiguated by Constraints) hold their meaning across FRs, the email table, and §3.

The two usability gaps are minor: (1) there is no formal Glossary section, so the consistent terminology is conventional rather than enforced — fine here because the role model in §3 effectively serves as one, but a UX/architecture author would benefit from an explicit term list; (2) the FR numbering uses deliberate gaps (F1 = FR-001..004, F2 = FR-010..016, with interpolations like FR-034b and FR-041a) — the gaps are clearly intentional spacing, not missing requirements, but a downstream tool that assumes contiguity could misread them.

### Findings
- **low** No explicit Glossary section (§3 carries the role vocabulary implicitly) — Terminology is consistent but not codified, so a UX/architecture extractor has no single source for domain nouns. *Fix:* Add a short Glossary (organizer capacity, attendee capacity, owning organizer, registered attendee, external registrant, blocked slot, registration close).

## Shape fit — strong

The PRD matches its product. This is a hybrid: an internal multi-stakeholder tool (organizers, admins) *plus* an external-facing consumer-style registration flow — and the shape correctly reflects that. The internal side is written as a capability spec (FR lists by feature area, operational SMs like "heatmap comprehension ≤ 30s"), which is right for staff users; the external registration side, which is genuinely consumer-facing, gets the UX-load-bearing treatment it needs (branded page FR-040, mobile/desktop equal priority NFR-004, WCAG 2.1 AA on public pages NFR-007, ≤2-minute completion target). It is not over-formalized: there are no ceremonial User Journeys with named protagonists, which would have been overhead for what is largely a single-operator-per-task internal tool, and the external flow is simple enough that the FR sequence (FR-040..047) functions as the journey. As a brownfield full-replacement, the existing-system references (§1, Constraints "Full replacement") are stated accurately and the new system is cleanly distinguished from the old. As a chain-top PRD, it correctly defers UX-specific detail to the named UX spec (NFR-006, NFR-007) rather than over-specifying. No shape-fit findings.

## Mechanical notes

- **Dangling ID reference:** FR-095 cites "resolves OQ-3" but the PRD contains no OQ-1/OQ-2/OQ-3 and no Open Questions section. This is the single clearest mechanical defect — the cross-reference does not resolve within the document.
- **FR numbering gaps are intentional, not errors:** Per-feature blocks are spaced (FR-001–004, FR-010–016, FR-020–023, FR-030–038, FR-040–047, FR-050–053, FR-060–063, FR-070–073, FR-080–083, FR-090–095, FR-100–105). Interpolated IDs FR-034b and FR-041a are unique and correctly placed; no duplicates observed.
- **Assumptions Index roundtrip:** N/A — there are no inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags and no index. (See Scope honesty: their absence is itself a finding.)
- **Glossary:** none present; domain nouns are nonetheless used consistently (no case/plural/synonym drift observed for "registered attendee," "owning organizer," "external registrant," "internal user").
- **Required sections for stakes/type:** Problem Statement, Goals & Metrics, Users & Roles, FRs, NFRs, Constraints, and Out-of-Scope are all present and appropriate for a chain-top brownfield replacement. Missing relative to the rubric's scaffolding: Open Questions, Assumptions Index, `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts, explicit Glossary.
- **UJ protagonists:** No formal UJs exist; given the shape (capacity spec + simple external flow) this is acceptable, so there are no floating/unnamed-protagonist UJs to flag.
